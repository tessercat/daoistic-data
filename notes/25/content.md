# Power

There was something whole and complete
before heaven and earth were born.
Still, silent,
eternal, timeless,
moving in endless cycles,
I think of it as the all-mother,
but I don't know its name.
有物混成
先天地生
寂兮寥兮
獨立不改
周行而不殆
可以為天下母
吾不知其名

They call it Dao
and they say it's great,
but greatness implies distance,
distance implies separation,
and separation implies rebellion.
字之曰道
強為之名曰大
大曰逝
逝曰遠
遠曰反

When Dao is great,
the heavens are great,
the earth is great,
and the king is great too.
Our homeland's greatness is four-fold,
but kingship embodies our common cause,
and humanity is to the earth,
as earth is to the heavens,
as the heavens are to Dao,
as Dao is to what is.
故道大
天大
地大
王亦大
域中有四大
而王居其一焉
人法地
地法天
天法道
道法自然


# Notes

https://ctext.org/dao-de-jing#n11594

To my mind,
the double meaning of 遠 as
separation and influence
gives the poem
a double meaning.

The author doesn't know 天下母's name (名)
but says (other) people give it a polite name (字) of 道
and a personal name (名) of 大,
and interpretation of the central triplet of 逝, 遠 and 反
flows from there.

First, it's a criticism of the "greatness" of kings.
Kings aren't great.
They unify us in a common purpose,
but when they lord over us,
the result is distance, estrangement and revolution.

But when kings aren't great,
they really do embody a common cause.
They're one of us,
and they naturally arise, exert influence and return,
just as the earth, the heavens and Dao
also move in endless cycles.

I'm not sure who's making the argument,
but I take it as
a not-so-subtle argument
to put kings in their place
not as permanent absolute authority,
but rather as a temporary measure,
and only as first among equals.

The Riverside Elder comments on 獨立 as 無匹雙,
having no twin or pair,
so I like it as
the self-perpetuating,
self-referencing,
recursive,
Ouroboros.
